How to choose a collagen supplements

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

To choose a collagen supplement, first decide between bovine and marine sources based on your dietary preferences and ethical concerns. Then look for hydrolyzed collagen peptides, a reputable third-party tested brand, and a form, powder or capsules, that fits your routine. Consistency matters more than fancy additives, so pick one you’ll actually take daily.

What types of collagen supplements are there?

The vast majority of collagen supplements come from one of two sources: bovine (cow) or marine (fish). Bovine collagen is rich in types I and III, which support skin, bones, and tendons. Marine collagen contains mostly type I, the primary collagen in skin, and is often praised for its smaller peptide size, which some believe improves absorption. There are also chicken-derived collagens (type II, popular for joint support) and even eggshell membrane collagen, but these are less common. Your choice should match your primary goal, skin elasticity, joint health, or general wellness, as well as any dietary restrictions (pescatarians lean toward marine, for example).

How do I choose between powder and capsules?

Powders offer flexibility: you can mix them into coffee, smoothies, or soups, and you can easily adjust your serving size. Capsules are convenient for travel or if you dislike the taste of collagen, but you’ll have to swallow multiple capsules to get a typical effective dose (most brands recommend several grams per day). Powders usually cost less per gram and allow you to control exactly how much you take. Capsules win on convenience and portability but often require a higher per-serving price to get the same collagen content. Think about your daily habits before buying.

What should I look for on the label?

First, make sure the label says “hydrolyzed collagen” or “collagen peptides.” This means the collagen has been broken down into smaller chains that your body can absorb more easily. Avoid products with a long list of artificial sweeteners, fillers, or proprietary blends that hide the actual collagen content. Third-party certification (like NSF, USP, or Informed Choice) signals that a product has been tested for purity and heavy metals, especially important for marine collagen, which can come from fish that may contain mercury. Also check the source: grass-fed bovine collagen is often pricier but may appeal if you care about animal welfare and sustainability.

Are there any downsides or side effects?

Collagen supplements are generally safe, but they’re not magical. Some people report mild digestive upset, bloating, or a feeling of fullness, especially when starting out. Powders can also clump or leave a slight aftertaste, though many dissolve well in hot liquids. Cost is another trade-off. Good-quality collagen supplements can be significantly more expensive than getting collagen from bone broth or skin-on chicken. And while studies show benefits for skin elasticity and joint comfort, results are modest, don’t expect dramatic overnight changes. Honest expectations help you avoid disappointment.

How long until I see results?

Most research on collagen supplementation shows noticeable improvements in skin hydration and elasticity after about 8 to 12 weeks of daily use. For joint comfort, some people report relief within a month, but others need several months. Consistency is the key. No supplement works if you skip days frequently. Make it part of your morning routine, like mixing a scoop into your coffee or tea. And remember that collagen is a protein, not a drug, your body uses its amino acids in many ways, so individual timelines vary.

Do I need vitamin C for collagen absorption?

Vitamin C is a necessary cofactor for your body to build its own collagen. Taking a vitamin C source at the same time as your supplement can help maximize the collagen synthesis process. Many collagen powders already include vitamin C in the formula, but if yours doesn’t, a squeeze of lemon juice or a glass of orange juice alongside your serving is a simple fix. That said, your body stores vitamin C, so as long as you eat fruits and vegetables regularly, you’re probably covered. Adding an extra boost won’t hurt, but it’s not mandatory for the supplement to work.

Frequently asked questions

Can collagen supplements really help with joint pain?

Several studies suggest that collagen peptides, particularly type II collagen from chicken, can reduce joint pain and improve mobility in conditions like osteoarthritis. Results are not universal, and relief is usually modest, but many active adults find it worthwhile.

Is marine collagen better than bovine collagen?

There’s no definitive answer. Marine collagen is often perceived as more sustainable and has smaller peptides, which some believe are more absorbable, but bovine collagen is backed by more long-term research. Your choice should depend on dietary preferences and budget, both can be effective.

Should I take collagen on an empty stomach?

Timing doesn’t seem to matter much. Some people prefer taking it with vitamin C for synergy, but there’s no strong evidence that an empty stomach improves absorption. Find a time you’ll remember, whether that’s with breakfast or before bed.

Are there common allergens in collagen supplements?

Yes. Marine collagen may contain fish or shellfish traces, and bovine collagen can trigger reactions in people with beef allergies. Always check the label for allergens, and look for products that are tested for common contaminants like lead or mercury.

Can I get enough collagen from food alone?

It’s possible but difficult to match the consistent dose you get from a supplement. Bone broth, skin-on chicken, and fish skin provide collagen, but you’d need to eat them daily in large amounts. A supplement is simply a more reliable and convenient source.

Do collagen powders expire?

Yes, most have a shelf life of 1 to 3 years when stored in a cool, dry place. Over time, the protein can degrade and lose potency. Always check the expiration date, and avoid leaving the powder in a humid bathroom.

In shortThe best collagen supplement is the one you can take consistently, from a transparent brand that offers third-party testing and a simple, effective formula. Match the source to your dietary needs, pick your preferred form, and give it a few months of daily use before judging the results.
